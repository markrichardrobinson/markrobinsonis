Church on the Edge of the Platform

Community · Faith · Everyday Grace

Most mornings the station feels like a moving crowd rather than a gathered community. Coffee cups, headphones, hurried steps, delays on the board. Every person focused on somewhere else.

Yet there are moments when something closer to church appears: someone lifts a suitcase for a stranger, a parent calms a child restless with noise and waiting, a guard repeats an announcement with real care because the last one sounded too harsh.

I have watched people stand shoulder to shoulder in that space while bad news travels through the trains: strikes, cancellations, accidents, storms. No one planned a liturgy, yet there is a strange shared rhythm: groans, laughter, complaints, phone calls, the quiet decision to stay in line rather than push.

When we speak about church, we often picture pews and pulpits. Platforms and buses rarely make the list. Still, many of the parables Jesus used grew out of crowded, ordinary places: marketplaces, fishing boats, fields. Somewhere people were already gathered, already tired, already hoping that today might run a little smoother than yesterday.

None of this excuses the injustices of our transport systems or the deep inequalities in who waits, who cleans, who repairs, who profits. Yet in the middle of that, small acts of care keep appearing: a seat offered, directions given, a shared joke when the screen changes again.

Church on the edge of the platform looks like this: people who do not know each other sharing a moment, an inconvenience, a delay, a story. In those unplanned gatherings, God is present, listening to the announcements and to the murmurs that rise beneath them, holding together a fragile community that will dissolve as soon as the next train arrives.
