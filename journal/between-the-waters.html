Journal

Between the Waters

Risk · Justice · Migration

There is a stretch of water that some maps mark as a border and others mark as a shipping lane. For many, it has become something else entirely: a graveyard and a narrow chance at a different future.

In the drawing that carries this title, a single boat moves between two shores. The lines stay simple. There are no flags, no coastguard vessels, no headlines circling overhead. Just a figure, a boat, deep water, and a horizon that refuses to explain itself.

We are used to hearing these crossings named with numbers: fatalities, rescues, interceptions. We are less used to hearing them named with family stories, languages, prayers whispered in the dark, songs remembered from childhood.

The Christian story knows something about dangerous waters: seas that part and close again, storms that threaten to sink everyone on board, rivers that need to be crossed for a people to reach a place of rest. In each of those stories, God is present in the risk rather than safely on the shore.

To stand on land and speak about “those people in the boats” is easy. To recognise that our policies, our borders, our comforts, and our fears help shape the waves they face is harder. The piece The Waters Between refuses to let that complexity drift out of sight.

Somewhere between one coast and another, a person sits in a boat, choosing every stroke of the oar, every breath. God is present there, between the waters, sharing each moment of risk, holding each story in a care that goes far deeper than any government will ever admit.
